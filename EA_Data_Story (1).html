End-to-End Analytics Case Study

From chaos
to clarity

One business problem. Three disconnected data sources. Six tools. A complete journey from raw, scattered data to clean insight, live dashboards, and concrete business recommendations — showing exactly how I approach analytics in the real world.

The Business

A European sales operation spanning 46 countries, 1,328 orders, and $1.70 billion in total revenue across online and offline channels.

The Problem

Data sitting in three separate systems — Sales, Logistics, and Finance — with no unified view, inconsistent formats, and no way to track performance in real time.

The Outcome

A clean, integrated dataset powering five live dashboards and five data-backed recommendations, all built from the same source of truth.

Stage 01: The Starting Point

Three sources.
No single truth.

Before any analysis could happen, the data had to be found, understood, and unified. Here's what we were working with — and why it was a problem.

📦

Sales Data

Order records, product IDs, unit prices, quantities, and revenue, but with inconsistent date formats and missing country codes for ~6% of orders.

Missing country codes Inconsistent dates Mixed case values
🚚

Logistics Data

Ship dates, delivery timelines, and order priority flags, stored separately with different Order IDs that needed reconciling against the sales records.

Separate ID system Unmatched priorities Duplicated rows
💰

Finance Data

Unit costs, total costs, and margin calculations, managed by a different team, in a different format, with some cost fields missing entirely.

Missing cost data Different currency handling No join key
↓

The goal: one clean, joined dataset — ready for every tool that follows.

Excel & Power Query

The three source files were merged using Power Query — joining on Order ID, standardising date formats, filling coded nulls with 'UNKNOWN', removing 2 rows with missing Units Sold, and correcting case inconsistencies in Sales Channel. Stage 03: What the Data Revealed

The numbers
don't lie.

Across all six tools and every angle of analysis, four findings came up consistently. These are the facts the business needs to act on.

52%
Revenue decline from peak

Revenue peaked at $285.5M in 2012 and has fallen to $136.6M by 2017 — a 52% decline in five years. The profit margin has held at ~29.5% throughout, which means the problem is volume, not pricing. Something structural changed in 2012–2013.

35.7%
Highest margin market (Denmark)

Denmark, Montenegro, UK, Monaco, and Switzerland all deliver margins above 34%, significantly above the 29.5% average. These markets are being underserved relative to their profitability potential. The business is over-indexed on high-volume, lower-margin countries.

0%
Priority pricing premium

Critical, High, Medium, and Low priority orders all carry the same ~29.5% margin. There is no pricing differentiation for urgency — a significant missed opportunity. Every industry charges a premium for critical/urgent orders. This business is leaving money on the table.

50/50
Online vs Offline split

The channel split is almost perfectly balanced (663 Online / 665 Offline), but Online delivers a slightly higher margin (29.9% vs 29.1%). The business has built a resilient dual-channel operation, but a modest shift toward Online would improve overall profitability without adding complexity.

Stage 04: What to Do About It

Five recommendations.
Backed by data.

These are not opinions. Every recommendation below is directly traceable to a specific finding in the data. This is what the analysis is for.

01

Investigate the
structural decline

Revenue fell 52% from its 2012 peak. The margin held — which rules out a pricing problem. This points to volume loss: fewer orders, smaller baskets, or market exit. A root cause analysis (win/loss data, churn by country, product mix shift) is the single most important action the business can take. Without understanding why, no other initiative will reverse the trend.

Priority: Critical, revenue is on a trajectory that makes the business unsustainable within 3–5 years
02

Introduce
priority-based pricing

Critical orders carry the same margin as Low priority orders. A 5% urgency premium on Critical orders (306 orders, $412M revenue) would generate an additional ~$6M in profit annually — with zero change to operations. Customers placing Critical orders are already signalling they need speed. They will pay for it.

Estimated impact: +$6M annual profit at 5% premium on Critical orders
03

Expand in
high-margin markets

Denmark (35.7%), Montenegro (35.6%), UK (35.0%), Monaco (34.7%), and Switzerland (34.6%) all outperform the portfolio average by 5+ percentage points. These markets are not receiving sales investment proportional to their profitability. Shifting even 10% of sales resource toward these markets would materially improve overall margin without increasing revenue.

Target: Grow top-5 margin markets from current share to 15% of total orders
04

Accelerate
the Online channel

Online margin (29.9%) outperforms Offline (29.1%) with identical order volumes. The infrastructure is already built, the channel is proven, balanced, and slightly more profitable. A deliberate shift from 50/50 to 55/45 in favour of Online (roughly 65 orders per year) would improve blended margin without adding headcount or operational complexity.

Estimated impact: +0.3–0.4% blended margin improvement at 55/45 split
05

Build a real-time monitoring infrastructure — and use it

The single biggest operational risk revealed by this analysis is that the revenue decline went undetected for years because the data lived in three disconnected systems. The dashboards built in this project — Power BI for operational monitoring, Tableau for executive storytelling, and the JavaScript tool for ad-hoc decisions — provide the infrastructure to catch problems early. The recommendation is simple: use them. Set monthly review cadences. Build alerts for YoY revenue drops over 10% by market. Make the data visible to the people who can act on it.

Outcome: A business that sees problems in weeks, not years
